Agentic Trading
— how it actually works

A friend sent you this. Here's the whole thing in plain English: it's a real, live experiment where an AI runs a small, high-risk trading account — and you're watching it think.

"If we ask how is that possible? instead of saying it's not — we usually find the answer."

1What this is

A real brokerage account with about $1,000 in it — money the owner has decided he can afford to lose entirely. An AI engine manages it, trying to grow it aggressively toward a wildly ambitious target (~$100,000). Think of it as a science experiment with real money, run out in the open. It is not investment advice, and the honest expectation is a small chance of a huge outcome and a large chance of losing the stake. That trade-off is the point, on purpose.

2What it's aiming for

Maximum growth, not safety. It buys leveraged ETFs — funds that move 2–3× as much as the market — to swing for big gains. That amplification cuts both ways, which is exactly why the rules below exist. The goal isn't to be right every day; it's to give a moonshot its best honest shot while never letting a single bad bet wipe out the whole account.

3How it thinks — every few hours

Four times a trading day, the engine wakes up and runs the same loop:

Reads the market. Prices, momentum, the news driving them.
Checks the "regime." Is the overall market trending up or down? Leverage only works with the trend — in a falling market these funds bleed value fast, so the honest move is to step aside.
Hunts for a setup. If the trend is up, it looks for the strongest names breaking out.
Applies survival rules (see below) and either proposes a trade or explains why it's sitting out.

So on any given day you'll see one of these:

a trade proposal → your phone "nothing worth it — holding cash, here's why" "armed a trigger — waiting for the level"

4How it stays out of trouble

You approve every real trade. The engine never spends real money on its own — it sends the idea to the owner's phone with an Approve / Skip button. Nothing happens without a human tap.

Every position has a hard stop. A pre-set exit that caps the loss on any one bet to a small slice of the account — so a single wrong call can't end the experiment.

Cash is a real position. When the market's ugly, doing nothing is the correct move, and the engine will happily do nothing and tell you why.

5How it learns from itself over time

This is the part that makes it more than a bot following rules. There's a genuine feedback loop:

Records — every decision goes into a detailed dated journal; every trade logs its reasoning, stop, and a pre-mortem of what could go wrong.
Scores — the account is measured against a dumb benchmark ("what if I'd just bought and held 3× the Nasdaq?"). If it can't beat that, the strategy isn't adding value.
Reviews — once a week it grades itself: which trades worked, which didn't, and what pattern keeps repeating.
Writes the lesson down — each real, evidence-backed takeaway is added to its own playbook, dated. (No made-up lessons — only things the data actually showed.)
Acts on it — and here's the key: the engine re-reads that playbook at the start of every single run. So a lesson learned on a Sunday changes how it trades on Monday. Outcomes → lessons → behavior. The loop closes.

6What you're looking at on the dashboard

The big number + chart — the live account value, like a stock app.

"What the engine's thinking" — short texts, like messages from a friend who watches the market all day, with timestamps. Even on days it does nothing, it tells you why.

Track Record — the honest scoreboard: is it beating buy-and-hold?

The Map — a little solar system of the names it's watching; the closer a planet orbits, the sooner its setup, and its size is how big a bet it would be.

Recent Trades / Open Positions — tap any of them to read the actual reasoning behind it.

The honest disclaimer: this is a personal, experimental account using money that can be fully lost. Leveraged trading is high-risk and most attempts to grow a small stake this fast end in a loss. Nothing here is financial advice — it's a real-money experiment shared in the open.
